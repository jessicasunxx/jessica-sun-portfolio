Soldering Sculpture

A small soldered sculpture on a protoboard where two looping brass rods rise from a 12-pin header, hinting at signal paths and stories I will explore throughout this class.

Materials

  • Small protoboard
  • 12-pin male header
  • Single brass rod, bent into two connected loops
  • Soldering iron and solder
  • Helping hands / third-hand tool
  • Flux, flush cutters, and pliers

Process

  • Soldered the 12-pin header cleanly into the protoboard, focusing on even, shiny solder beads.
  • Planned a simple but expressive form made from a single brass rod forming two looping arcs.
  • Bent the rod gradually with pliers, testing how far it could lean without pulling the header off the board.
  • Soldered the rod into selected header pins one joint at a time, adjusting the loops until the piece felt balanced and intentional.
  • Cleaned excess flux, checked each joint for strength, and photographed the sculpture against a simple background to highlight its silhouette.

Reflection

This project turned soldering from a purely functional skill into a way of sketching in three dimensions. Limiting myself to a single brass rod and a tiny protoboard made every bend and every joint feel important, because a small change could shift the balance or break the clean line of the loops.

I learned how much control it takes to place heat and solder exactly where I want it without re-melting previous joints, and how tools like helping hands quietly shape what forms are even possible. The two loops feel like parallel signal paths or ideas that start from the same point and diverge, which is how I think about my work in this class: branching experiments grounded in the same hardware skills. In future pieces, I want to build on this by layering more complex forms and eventually embedding actual electronics into the structures.
